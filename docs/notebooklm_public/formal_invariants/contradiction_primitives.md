# Contradiction Primitives

Contradiction primitives expose recurring logical tensions in runtime legitimacy systems.

## Primitive 1: Identity-Legitimacy Split

Formal statement:
A mutation may remain identical while becoming illegitimate.

Explanation:
Identity continuity of action does not guarantee continuity of admissibility context.

## Primitive 2: Historical-Computational Split

Formal statement:
An approval may remain historically valid while becoming computationally invalid.

Explanation:
Record truth and execution truth can diverge under runtime drift.

## Primitive 3: Replay-Authority Split

Formal statement:
Replayable execution may remain unauthorized.

Explanation:
Reproducibility does not imply present authority continuity.

## Primitive 4: Visibility-Legitimacy Split

Formal statement:
Observability may confirm execution while failing to validate legitimacy.

Explanation:
Detection and admissibility are different governance functions.

## Primitive 5: Determinism-Authority Split

Formal statement:
Deterministic execution may still violate runtime authority continuity.

Explanation:
Determinism controls procedural consistency, not authority correctness.

## Primitive 6: Approval-Commit Split

Formal statement:
Approval correctness at T0 does not imply commit correctness at T1.

Explanation:
Temporal divergence separates decision context from execution context.

## Primitive 7: Capability-Intent Split

Formal statement:
Execution capability can expand while approval intent remains narrow.

Explanation:
Intent artifacts can become misaligned with evolved consequence surfaces.

## Use in Legitimacy Theory

These primitives help formalize why approval-centric models break under autonomous execution: they expose where continuity assumptions silently fail.

## Closing View

Contradictions are not anomalies; they are structural outcomes of mutable distributed runtime systems.
